Lost in Translation:
The Impact of Language of Instruction on PISA Outcomes

The Philippines vs. Top-Performing Nations

Standardized testing is designed to measure student capability, but what happens when the test itself speaks a foreign language? This project engages in a multidimensional comparison to explore how language policies in assessments shape educational outcomes, focusing on the cognitive gap experienced by non-native English speakers.

Analytical Framework: Bray & Thomas (1995)

To ensure a structured and comprehensive comparative analysis, this research utilizes the Bray & Thomas framework (Bray & Thomas, 1995). By integrating a cross-national comparison and a comparative policy analysis, we examine how national education systems operate and are shaped by external and internal factors.

01

Context: The Environment Shaping the System

The external environment heavily dictates the trajectory of an education system.

Cultural

Societal values and colonial history deeply influence language preferences in education. In the Philippines, English is culturally viewed as the language of success, whereas countries like Finland emphasize learning in the student's mother tongue.

Political

Government policies dictate the medium of instruction. We compare the decentralized language policies of top PISA performers with the bilingual/English-centric approach of the Philippine curriculum.

Social

Education plays a vital role in social mobility. When the language of instruction alienates the masses, social stratification widens.

02

Inputs: Resources Injected into the System

The quality of educational delivery depends on the resources allocated to the system.

Socioeconomic Status (SES)

Lower SES students often have less exposure to English outside of school compared to their wealthier peers. When the test is in English, this socioeconomic gap is multiplied by a linguistic disadvantage, punishing students not just for poverty, but for lack of English immersion.

Human Resources & Teacher Training

Teachers themselves may struggle with English proficiency. If a science teacher is anxious about teaching complex concepts in an unnatural language, pedagogical quality drops, shifting focus from "scientific understanding" to "English translation survival."

Material Resources

A critical gap exists in the availability of educational materials. Developing comprehensive textbooks translated into local dialects requires deep financial and time investments that many developing nations struggle to allocate.

03

Processes: Delivery and Assessment

Processes refer to the ways in which education is delivered and how learning occurs.

Pedagogy

Teaching methods vary significantly when a language barrier is present. Educators often shift from interactive, student-centered learning to lecture-based rote memorization to compensate for vocabulary deficits.

Curriculum

The structure and delivery of the curriculum must align with the linguistic realities of the student body.

Assessment

The methods used to evaluate student performance are the core of this issue. Standardized testing like PISA often measures a student's English reading comprehension simultaneously with their scientific or mathematical aptitude, creating a heavy cognitive load.

Outcomes: The Results of the Education System

Student Achievement

Academic performance and literacy levels in the Philippines show a direct correlation with the language used in assessments.

Economic Outcomes

The economic impact of education is hindered when workforce development is compromised by foundational learning gaps.

Social Outcomes

Ultimately, lower academic achievement limits global employment opportunities and civic engagement.

WHAT IF

The Ripple Effect: A Higher PISA Ranking

Using the Bray & Thomas framework, what if the Philippines removed the language barrier and climbed the PISA ranks? Here are realistic projections across different contexts.

Cultural Shift

Projection: The stigma surrounding local dialects in academic settings would diminish. Culturally, intelligence would no longer be exclusively associated with English fluency, fostering a stronger national identity and intellectual confidence among students.

Political Capital

Projection: A surge in PISA rankings would validate localized education policies. The government would gain the political capital to secure higher educational budgets (Inputs) and attract international investments, shifting focus from "basic, language-based outsourcing" to "high-skilled innovation."

Social Mobility

Projection: With foundational science and math skills mastered in their mother tongue, lower SES students could compete more equitably for STEM scholarships and university admissions, significantly flattening the social stratification curve.

Conclusion

Through this multidimensional comparison, it becomes evident that a student's capacity to learn and perform is intrinsically linked to the language of instruction and assessment. To foster a truly global perspective on education, policies must ensure that assessments are a bridge to demonstrating knowledge, not a linguistic barrier.
